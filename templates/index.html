Stress

Stress is your body's reaction to a challenge or demand. In short bursts, stress can be positive, such as when it helps you avoid danger or meet a deadline. But when stress lasts for a long time, it may harm your health.

There are two main types of stress:

  • Acute: This is short-term stress that goes away quickly. It also occurs when you do something new or exciting. All people have acute stress at one time or another.
  • Chronic: This is stress that lasts for a longer period of time. You may have chronic stress if you have money problems, an unhappy marriage, or trouble at work.

Stress Management

    Short term management:
  • Meditate
  • Practice Progressive Muscle Relaxation: It involves relaxing all the muscles in your body, group by group.
  • Focus on Breathing
  • Take a Walk
  • Create Artwork
    • Long Term management
  • Eat a Balanced Diet
  • Make Time for Leisure Activities
  • Develop a Positive Self-Talk Habit
  • Practice Yoga
  • Prioritize Exercise
Anxiety

Anxiety is a mental health disorder characterised by feelings of worry or fear that are strong enough to inhibit with one's daily activities. Anxiety is our body's most common response to stress and depression.

Anxiety Management

    Self treatment
  • First, understand it is not you fault to get anxious. So move away from it's crippling effects on your life.
  • Stress Management: Managing stress can help limit potential triggers. Organize your daily work and try to decrease pressures.
  • Physical Exercise: Physical exertion can improve self-image and release negative thoughts.
  • Seek support: Talk with trusty people who are supportive.
    Counselling
  • Psychotherapy: Also known as talking therapy, some options include one-to-one counseling and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).
Grief

Grief is a natural response to loss. It’s the emotional suffering you feel when something or someone you love is taken away. The pain of loss is high. You may suffer to come out of those situations and their shock.

Stages of grief

  1. Denial
  2. Anger
  3. Depression
  4. Acceptance

Grief Management

  • Seek help: The most important thing to do when you're suffering from grievance is seeking support from your family members or a person who's supportive to overcome the loss and grief.
  • Face your feelings: You can try to supress your grief, but you can't avoid it forever. Better try to face and acknowledge the pain.
  • Professional help: If you’re experiencing symptoms of complicated grief, talk to a mental health professional right away.

Warning

Left untreated, complicated grief can lead to significant emotional damage, life-threatening health problems, and even suicide. But treatment can help you get better.

Trauma

A traumatic event is an incident that causes physical, emotional, spiritual, or psychological harm

The person experiencing the distressing event may feel physically threatened or extremely frightened as a result.The person will need support and time to recover from the traumatic event and regain emotional and mental stability.

Some Examples of traumatic events are:
  • Physical pain or injury
  • Serious illness
  • War/Terrorism
  • Natural disasters
  • Abuse

Trauma Management

  • Communicate the experience with family or close friends or in a diary or online journal
  • Pursue hobbies or other interests.
  • Find a support group led by a trained professional who can facilitate discussions.
  • Take a timeout
  • Try to eat a well-balanced diet, exercise, get adequate rest, and avoid alcohol and drugs.
  • Maintain a daily routine with structured activities.
  • Spend time with others to avoid becoming withdrawn, even if you do not feel up to it.
Anger

Anger is a natural response to perceived threats. It causes your body to release adrenaline, your muscles to tighten, and your heart rate and blood pressure to increase. Your senses might feel more acute and your face and hands flushed.

Being angry isn't always a bad thing. Being angry can help you share your concerns. It can prevent others from walking all over you. It can motivate you to do something positive. The key is managing your anger in a healthy way.

Anger Management

  • Think before you speak
  • Once you're calm, express your anger
  • Get some exercise
  • Take a timeout
  • Identify possible solutions to your problem
  • Identify possible solutions
  • Don't hold a grudge
  • Use humor to release tension
  • Practice relaxation skills: like meditation, breathing exercises, yoga
